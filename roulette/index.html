How to play roulette

European roulette is a casino classic. A wheel with 37 numbered pockets — 0 through 36 — spins one way; a small ball spins the other. Bet on where the ball lands before the croupier closes the table. When the ball settles, winning bets pay automatically.

You can wager on a single number (straight, 35:1), pairs and lines (split 17:1, street 11:1, corner 8:1), columns or dozens (2:1), or even-money outside bets like red/black, odd/even, and high/low (1:1). Stack any combination across the layout — there's no limit on how many bets per spin.

The single zero pocket is what gives the house its edge. On every bet, the math returns about 97.3 cents on the dollar long-term, leaving a 2.7% house edge — half the edge of American roulette, which adds a 00 pocket.

Tips & strategy

  • Stick to European, not American. Half the house edge for the same game.
  • Outside bets last longer. Red/black wins about 48.6% of the time. Great for slow grinding.
  • Inside bets pay big but lose often. A straight number hits once every 37 spins on average.
  • No betting system fixes the house edge. Martingale, Fibonacci, D'Alembert — all eventually lose to the math.
  • The wheel has no memory. Red five times in a row doesn't make black "due." Every spin is independent.
  • Set a session bankroll and a stop-loss. Bankroll discipline is the only real edge.

About roulette

Roulette — French for "little wheel" — was invented in 17th-century France, with the modern single-zero layout established by François and Louis Blanc in the 1840s. The double-zero American version emerged later in U.S. casinos. The game's mathematical purity (every spin truly independent, every bet's expectation calculable) makes it a favorite of probability writers and statistics teachers alike.

Frequently asked questions

Is this roulette game free?

Yes — free play, no real money. Your bankroll and stats save in your browser.

Is this European or American roulette?

European — a single zero. The house edge is 2.7%, half of American roulette's 5.26%.

Can I bet on multiple things at once?

Yes. Stack any combination of inside and outside bets across the layout.

Does Martingale actually work?

No. Doubling after each loss requires unlimited bankroll and no table limits — neither exists.

Are spins truly random?

Yes. Each spin uses an independent random outcome. Past results have zero influence on the next — there are no "due" numbers.
