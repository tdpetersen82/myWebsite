How to play blackjack

Blackjack is a card game between you and the dealer. Build a hand closer to 21 than the dealer's — without going over. Number cards score at face value, face cards count 10, and an ace is worth 1 or 11, whichever helps you most.

Place a bet to start the round. You and the dealer each get two cards; yours are face-up, the dealer's second card stays hidden. Then choose your action:

  • Hit — take another card. Keep hitting until you stand or bust.
  • Stand — keep your total. The dealer plays next.
  • Double — double your bet for one and only one more card. Best on 9, 10, or 11.
  • Split — when dealt a pair, split it into two hands with a new second card on each. Each hand plays independently.

If your total goes over 21, you bust and lose the bet immediately. Once you finish, the dealer flips their hidden card and draws until they reach 17 or more — our table uses the player-friendly Stand-on-Soft-17 rule. Beat the dealer's total and you win even money. Land a natural blackjack (ace plus a 10-value card on the first two cards) and you win 3:2.

Tips & strategy

  • Always split aces and eights. Aces give you two shots at 21; two eights make a weak 16 that's almost always better split into two hands.
  • Never split fives or tens. Two fives total a strong 10 — better to double. Splitting tens breaks up a 20, the second-best hand in the game.
  • Double on 11. One card away from 21 with a four-in-thirteen chance of getting it. Double whenever the dealer shows 2 through 10.
  • Hit 12–16 when the dealer shows 7+. A strong dealer up-card will likely make 17–21 anyway. Take the risk.
  • Stand on 12–16 when the dealer shows 2–6. Weak up-cards bust often. Let them.
  • Skip insurance. The math doesn't work — long-term it's a losing side bet.
  • Never deviate from basic strategy on a hunch. Every chart move is the mathematically best decision given the cards. Hunches cost money.

About blackjack

Blackjack traces to 18th-century French casinos as Vingt-et-Un — "twenty-one." It crossed to America in the 1800s and exploded in popularity through the 20th century, becoming the most-played table game in the world. Ed Thorp's 1962 book Beat the Dealer proved a skilled player could beat the house by counting cards, kicking off both a cultural fascination and a long cat-and-mouse game with casinos. Today blackjack remains one of the lowest-house-edge games in the casino — basic strategy gets you down to about a 0.5% disadvantage.

What's a "soft" hand?

A hand with an ace counted as 11. For example, ace + six is a soft 17. If hitting would bust the soft total, the ace flips to 1 and you keep playing.

Should I take insurance?

No. Insurance pays 2:1 but the dealer hits blackjack only about 30% of the time, so the bet loses money in the long run.

Can I count cards here?

Not effectively — the shoe is reshuffled often enough that counting won't give you a meaningful edge. Use it to practice basic strategy instead.

Does the dealer hit on soft 17?

No. Our table uses the player-friendly Stand on Soft 17 (S17) rule. The dealer hits any hard total of 16 or less and stands on any 17 or higher.
