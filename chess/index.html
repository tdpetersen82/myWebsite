Chess rules — how the pieces move

White moves first, then players alternate. Each piece travels its own way:

  • Pawn — forward one square (or two from its starting row), and captures one square diagonally. Reach the far end and it promotes, usually to a queen.
  • Knight — an L-shape: two squares one way, then one at a right angle. The only piece that leaps over others.
  • Bishop — any distance diagonally.
  • Rook — any distance straight, along ranks or files.
  • Queen — any distance straight or diagonal; the most powerful piece.
  • King — one square in any direction. If it's attacked that's check and you must respond; if it can't escape, that's checkmate and the game is over.

Three special moves round out the rules: castling (the king and a rook move together to tuck the king into safety), en passant (a pawn capturing a pawn that just slid past it), and promotion.

Chess strategy for beginners

  • Control the center. Pieces aimed at the four central squares command the most board.
  • Develop early. Get your knights and bishops into play before launching an attack.
  • Castle. Move your king to safety in the opening — don't leave it stuck in the middle.
  • Don't hang pieces. Before every move, check what your opponent's last move now attacks.
  • Trade when ahead. A material lead is easiest to convert with fewer pieces on the board.

Playing against the computer

Easy is a forgiving introduction for new players, Medium punishes loose moves, and Hard calculates several moves deep — it will pounce on a hanging piece or an exposed king. Switch the board color, piece set, and coordinates to taste; your choices are remembered between visits.

Frequently asked

How do I play Chess? Move one piece per turn according to how it travels, working to checkmate the opponent's king — trapping it so it can't avoid capture.

How do I get better at Chess? Control the center, develop your pieces, castle early, and check for threats before each move. Then play often — pattern recognition comes from games, not theory.
