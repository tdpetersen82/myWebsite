How to play Chess

Chess is the two-player strategy game where you maneuver six piece types around an 8×8 board with one goal: trap your opponent's king in checkmate. Each piece moves in its own pattern — pawns advance, knights jump in an L-shape, bishops slide diagonally, rooks slide in straight lines, the queen does both, and the king moves one square in any direction. Capture by moving onto a square the other side occupies.

Controls

  • Click a piece, then click a destination square
  • Green dots show legal squares; rings show captures
  • The AI plays the opposite color and replies automatically after your move
  • Use the Difficulty dropdown anytime — the AI adopts the new tier on its next move
  • Hit Switch sides to swap colors and let the AI continue from your position

Difficulty tiers

  • Easy — depth 2 with jittered move choice. Great for learning the rules.
  • Medium — depth 3. Plays solidly; a strong club player can still beat it.
  • Hard — depth 4 plus quiescence search on captures. Targets roughly 1200–1500 club strength.

All standard rules are enforced: castling on both sides, en passant, pawn promotion with a piece picker, check, checkmate, stalemate, plus 50-move and threefold-repetition draws. Your wins, losses, and best streak save locally.

Tips & strategy

  • Control the center. The four central squares (d4, d5, e4, e5) give pieces the most mobility — lose the center and you lose tempo.
  • Develop before you attack. Knights and bishops out first, castle early, and don't move the same piece twice in the opening without a reason.
  • Look for forks, pins, and skewers. Tactical shots win more club-level games than deep positional plans.
  • Don't trade into a worse structure. A trade that doubles your pawns and undoubles theirs is a positional loss even when material is even.
  • Improve your worst piece. When you're stuck for a move, find the piece doing the least work and give it a job.

About Chess

Chess descends from the 7th-century Indian game chaturanga, traveled west through Persia (where the cry "shāh māt" — the king is helpless — became checkmate), and reached Europe by the 10th century. The modern queen and bishop moves are 15th-century European additions. FIDE has governed competitive chess since 1924. Top human players rate around 2850 Elo today; the strongest engines exceed 3500.
