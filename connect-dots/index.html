How to play Dots & Boxes

Dots & Boxes is the classic pencil-and-paper game on a grid of dots. You and the computer take turns drawing one line between two adjacent dots. Complete the fourth side of a box and you claim it — and you get another turn. Whoever claims the most boxes when all lines are drawn wins.

Controls

  • Click or tap between two adjacent dots — draw a line there
  • New game — restart with a fresh grid

Difficulty

  • Easy — 3×3 boxes, mostly-random computer.
  • Medium — 4×4 boxes, avoids handing you boxes.
  • Hard — 5×5 boxes, plays safe and gives the shortest chain when forced.

Tips & strategy

  • Avoid completing the third side of a box. Drawing the third side hands your opponent the fourth-side claim plus a free extra turn. Count three-sided boxes before every move.
  • Sacrifice short chains. When you're forced to give a chain, give the shortest one — longer chains hand more boxes to your opponent.
  • The double-cross trick. When taking a long chain, leave the last two boxes for your opponent — they're forced to give you the next chain. This is the key endgame technique.
  • Plan parity. In the opening, the player who makes the first chain often loses. Count grid squares and adjust to force your opponent to break first.

About Dots & Boxes

Dots and Boxes was first described in 1889 by French mathematician Édouard Lucas under the name La Pipopipette. It's a deceptively deep combinatorial game — modern computers can solve only modestly-sized boards. The double-cross technique was popularized by mathematician Elwyn Berlekamp's 2000 book The Dots and Boxes Game: Sophisticated Child's Play.
